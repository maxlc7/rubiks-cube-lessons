The Matched Yellow Cross

You've seen this exact problem before — on the white cross. Now you solve it one floor up, with a slide.

From Lesson 7: you made the yellow cross — all four top edges showing yellow up. But look at their other colors: the side stickers don't line up with the centers yet. Keep white down, yellow up.

Remember Lesson 5? Your white cross had yellow-up… no — your white cross had white-up but the second colors didn't match the centers. Same situation now, flipped to the top. Back then you could just lift an edge and drop it in. You can't do that here — dropping an edge would smash the two solved layers underneath. So instead of moving one piece, you slide three at once with an algorithm.
The Big Idea

You can't disturb the layers below, so you rotate three top edges among themselves — a 3-cycle — to walk each one to where its side color matches its center. The 4th edge sits safely at the back.

The tool: a 3-edge cycle

Hold the cube with a solved edge at the back (more on finding one below). This algorithm cycles the other three edges (front, left, right) around, leaving the back one and both lower layers untouched:1

The edge 3-cycle
R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2
say it in chunks: (R U′) (R U) (R U) (R U′) · R′ U′ R2
back edge = fixed   the other 3 (front / left / right) rotate among themselves
Why it's safe. A 3-cycle only shuffles three pieces in a ring and puts everything else back — the back edge, all the corners, and both finished layers don't move. It also doesn't flip any edge, so your yellow stays facing up. Pure permutation (moving), zero orientation change — the cleanest example yet of that Lesson 1 split.1

How to use it: find a match, park it at the back

  1. Turn only the top (U) and watch the four side colors. Stop when at least one edge matches its center (that side now shows a solid block of color top-to-bottom).
  2. Put that solved edge at the back. Turn the whole cube so the matched side faces away from you.
  3. Run the 3-cycle. It spins the other three toward their homes.
  4. Re-check. All four matched? Done. If not, find the solved edge again, park it at the back, and run it once more. Two passes is the most you'll ever need.
solved edge → goes to the BACK
A "solved edge" is one whose side colour already matches its centre (ringed). Park THIS one at the back so the 3-cycle spins the other three home.

Hands-on: match the whole cross ~5 min

  1. White down, yellow up. Spin the U layer and find a position where at least one edge matches its center.
  2. Turn the whole cube so that matched edge is at the back.
  3. Run R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2.
  4. Re-check and repeat if needed (find the solved edge, back, run again).
  5. Matched yellow cross. All four sides now show their center color running up into the top edge. The corners are still a mess — that's the next two lessons.

What you just learned: the 3-cycle — the workhorse of finishing a cube. Move three, leave the rest. You'll use the same idea on the corners.

Snag — no matter how I turn the top, NO edge matches

That's the "two opposite edges swapped" case. Just run the algorithm from any angle once — it'll create a matched edge. Then do the normal routine: solved edge to the back, run again. Two passes total and you're matched.1
