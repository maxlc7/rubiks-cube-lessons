Rubik's Cube · Lesson 3

The White Cross

First real progress on the cube in your hand. No memorized algorithm — pure reasoning.

New reality: your cube is scrambled, and the goal is now plain — solve this one. You've got the two tools you need: you see pieces not stickers (Lesson 1), and you can name moves with U D F B R L and primes (Lesson 2). Time to use them.

Why this lesson? Every solving method builds the cube one layer at a time, and it starts here. The white cross is the only stage you can solve by pure intuition — no algorithm to memorize — which makes it the perfect place to feel what "delivering a piece to its home" actually means with your hands. Get this and you've broken the seal on solving.

The whole road — so you can see where you are

The beginner method solves the cube in 6 stages, bottom layer to top.1 You don't need to hold all of this — just see the shape of the journey:

  1. White cross — the 4 white edges, matched to side centers you are here
  2. White corners — finish the entire first layer
  3. Middle layer — the 4 edges around the equator
  4. Yellow cross — flip the last-layer edges so yellow faces up
  5. Orient last layer — get all yellow on top
  6. Permute last layer — slide the final pieces into their homes. Solved.
The Big Idea

A white edge is "home" only when its white sticker points up and its other color matches the center it sits against. Both, or it's in the wrong slot.

White up is not enough — the side colour decides the home. The ringed red sticker must sit against the red centre; over any other centre it's a fake cross, even with white pointing up.

This is Lesson 1's place-vs-orientation idea made real. Each white edge has two colors — say white+red. Its one true home is between the white center and the red center. If you just jam any white sticker on top without checking the second color, you've oriented it but mis-placed it — a fake cross. Beginners make this exact mistake and then can't finish. You won't.

Why white first, and why a cross? The white center is bolted to the core (Lesson 1) — it's a fixed anchor you can build against. And the 4 white edges form a cross because each edge lives between two centers; placed correctly they radiate out from the white center like spokes, each spoke's side color flowing into the matching face. The cross is just "all four white edges, home."

The method — white center on top

Hold your cube with the white center facing up the whole time. We'll lift white edges up from the bottom into the cross, one at a time.1

Hands-on: build your first cross ~8 min · real progress

  1. Find a white edge in the bottom layer. Look around the bottom for any edge piece with a white sticker on it. (An edge has 2 colors — Lesson 1.)
  2. Read its other color. Say the edge is white+blue. The blue sticker is the one that has to match a center.
  3. Park it under its matching center. Turn the bottom layer (D or D') until that edge sits directly below the blue side center. Now its home is straight above it.
  4. Lift it into the cross. Turn that whole face 180° — e.g. if blue is the front, do F2. The white edge swings up to the top, white pointing up, blue matching the blue center. One spoke done.
  5. Repeat for all 4 white edges. Each time, park under the matching center, then turn that face 180° to lift it up. Work around so a finished spoke doesn't get knocked back down.
  6. Check your cross. White plus sign on top, and — looking at the sides — each edge's second color flows straight into its matching center. That's a true cross.

What you just did: delivered four pieces to their homes by reasoning about their colors — no algorithm, no memorization. That's the whole game, in miniature.

Snag 1 — the white edge isn't in the bottom layer

If a white edge is stuck in the top or middle, turn a face to drop it down into the bottom layer first, then solve it the normal way. Don't fight it up top — get it to the bottom and run the 4 steps.

Snag 2 — the edge is in the bottom but white points sideways (flipped)

If the white sticker faces out instead of down, the simple 180° lift would put white on the side, not the top. Park it under its matching center anyway, then use a single quarter-turn plus a small adjustment to feed it in white-up. Easiest fix: just turn that face once (F) to bring it to the middle, line it up, and tuck it in — or ask me and I'll walk the exact turns for your specific edge.

When the red-ringed white sticker faces out to the side instead of down, a plain 180° lift would land white on the side, not the top. Re-orient the edge first, then drop it in white-up.

3. Why can you solve the white cross by intuition, with no memorized algorithm?
Why it gets harder after this (and that's the point). The cross is intuitive because the edges have nowhere to interfere. Soon, placing one piece will disturb others — and that's exactly when we'll need algorithms: short move-strings (like the sexy move you felt in Lesson 2) engineered to move a few pieces while putting everything else back. You'll understand each one as "a controlled cycle," not a spell. That starts in Lesson 4.
