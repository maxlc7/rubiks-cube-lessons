Rubik's Cube · Lesson 6

The Middle Layer

Two layers down. Now you fill the belt around the middle — with one algorithm and its mirror image.

From Lessons 4 & 5: you built a true matched cross and seated all four white corners. The entire first layer is solid — white face complete, a matching color band wrapping all four sides. Flip nothing: keep white on the bottom, yellow on top.

Why this lesson? The middle layer is the cube's "equator" — the four edges that sit between your finished corners. Here you stop hoping pieces fall into place and learn to deliberately insert one, choosing left or right by reading the piece. You'll also meet a beautiful idea that runs the rest of the cube: an algorithm and its mirror do the same job in opposite directions.
The Big Idea

The four middle edges are the only edges with no yellow. Find one up top, aim it at the slot it belongs in, and decide one thing: does it go left or right?

Yellow is your "not yet" flag. Every middle-layer edge has two colors and neither is yellow (yellow edges belong in the last layer, so leave them on top). So your first move each time is a filter: spot a top edge with no yellow — that's a middle piece looking for its home.

The decision: left or right?

Hold the cube white-down, yellow-up. Pick a top edge with no yellow. It has a color on the side (facing you, on the front) and a color on top. Do this:

  1. Turn the top (U) until the edge's front color matches the front center. Now it makes a little vertical bar of one color down the front face — a "T".
  2. Read the edge's TOP color. That color belongs to a side center — and it's either the center on your right or the one on your left. That's the direction the edge needs to travel.

The two algorithms (one is just the mirror of the other)

With the edge's front color matched to the front center, choose by the top color's direction:

▶ Insert to the RIGHT
U R U' R' U' F' U F
◀ Insert to the LEFT
U' L' U L U F U' F'
Look closely — they're reflections. The left algorithm is the right one held up to a mirror: every R becomes L, every turn flips its direction (U ↔ U'), and front stays front. You're not memorizing two unrelated strings — you're learning one move and its reflection. That mirror trick comes back again and again on the last layer.1
Why it doesn't wreck the layers below. Same logic as your corner move, just longer: the sequence briefly lifts a first-layer pair up top, swaps your edge in, then puts the borrowed pair back exactly where it was. Almost everything cancels; the one lasting change is your edge dropping into the belt. A scalpel, not a sledgehammer.1

Hands-on: fill all four belt edges ~10 min · three layers DONE

  1. White down, yellow up. Scan the top layer for an edge with no yellow.
  2. Match its front color to the front center with U turns (make the colored bar / "T").
  3. Read its top color → decide left or right.
  4. Run the matching algorithm (right or left) once. The edge screws down into the belt; the layers below come back intact.
  5. Repeat for all four belt edges. When you run out of no-yellow edges on top, check the snag below.
  6. Two layers fully solved. The bottom two-thirds of every face is one solid color. Only the yellow top layer remains.

What you just learned: deliberate insertion — read a piece, choose a direction, deploy the matching tool. And that algorithms come in mirror pairs.

First — are you already done? When every edge left on top has yellow on it AND the bottom two rows of all four sides are solid blocks of their center color, your middle layer is finished — those yellow edges belong to the last layer. Running out of no-yellow edges up top isn't a problem, it's the finish line. Only reach for the snag below if a side's solid block is broken by an off-color square sitting in the middle row.

Snag — a middle edge is stuck in a belt slot the wrong way (a side's block is broken)

That means a middle edge is stuck in a belt slot the wrong way (flipped or in the wrong home). You can't fix it from up top, so kick it out: put any spare top edge above that bad slot, hold the slot at front-right, and run the right-insert once. It boots the wrong edge up to the top layer — now match it and insert it properly.1

It splits into two jobs you'll learn to read: first orient the last layer (get all yellow facing up), then permute it (slide the pieces to their correct seats).
