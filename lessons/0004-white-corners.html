Rubik's Cube · Lesson 4

The White Corners

You finish the first layer — and meet your first real algorithm. One you've already felt.

From Lesson 3: you built a true white cross by reasoning — four edges home, side colors matched. The first layer is almost done. What's left are the four white corners that fill the gaps between your cross edges.

Why this lesson? Corners are where intuition runs out — placing one without a tool tends to wreck your cross. So this is the moment an algorithm earns its keep: a short, memorized move-string that does one controlled job and puts everything else back. Your mission is to understand why the algorithm works, not chant it — and you're perfectly set up, because you already discovered this exact sequence with your own hands.
You've met this move before. In Lesson 2 you did R U R' U' six times and the cube returned to solved. That told you it's a controlled cycle — it disturbs only a handful of pieces and always comes back around. Now you'll aim that cycle at a single corner and ride it home. Same move, real job.
First, flip the cube over. For this stage, turn the whole cube so your white cross is on the BOTTOM (white down, yellow center up).1 Why? We build the finished first layer on the bottom and use the open top (yellow) layer as a free staging area to maneuver corners — without ever touching the layer we've already solved. From here on, "U" means the yellow top.
The Big Idea

A white corner has 3 colors and exactly one home — the single bottom slot where all three colors meet their matching centers. Park it in the top right above that slot, then screw it in.

The algorithm — the "right-hand 4-move"

Hold the cube with white on the bottom. The insertion tool is:1

R U R' U'

Read it as a little story about the front-right-bottom slot (your target): R lifts the slot's contents up into the top; U spins the top to swap in your waiting corner; R' brings the column back down; U' tidies the top back. One pass takes the corner up, turns it, and sets it back down a little differently. Repeat it and the corner screws into place — white landing on the bottom — in at most 3 passes.

Why it doesn't wreck your cross. Every R is later undone by an R', and every U by a U' — it's nearly a complete self-cancel (that's why 6 reps returned to solved in Lesson 2). The only thing that doesn't fully cancel is the one corner riding in the front-right. So the sequence is a scalpel: it changes that corner and leaves the rest — including your whole cross — untouched.

Hands-on: fill all four corners ~10 min · first layer DONE

  1. Cube on white-bottom, yellow-up. Look at the top (yellow) layer and find a corner piece that has white on it. (A corner has 3 colors — Lesson 1.)
  2. Read its other two colors and find where it belongs: the bottom slot sitting between those two matching side centers. Example: a white+green+orange corner belongs where the green and orange faces meet.
  3. Park it above its slot. Turn just the top (U) layer until that white corner sits in the top, directly above its target slot.
  4. Hold the target slot at front-right, then do R U R' U'. Look. Not in yet? Do it again. Repeat (max 3×) until the corner clicks home: white on the bottom, both side colors matching.
  5. Repeat for all 4 white corners. Each time: find a white corner up top → park above its slot → hold that slot front-right → 4-move until seated.
  6. First layer complete. The entire white face is solid white, and the top row of all four sides shows a matching band of color. That's two full layers' worth of pieces locked in.

What you just learned: an algorithm isn't magic — it's a sequence engineered to mostly cancel itself, leaving one tiny, intended change. You'll reuse this exact idea for the rest of the solve.

Snag — a white corner is stuck in the BOTTOM, in the wrong spot or twisted

If a white corner is jammed in a bottom slot incorrectly, you can't fix it from up top. Pop it out: hold that bad slot at front-right and do R U R' U' once — it kicks the corner up into the top layer. Now solve it normally.1
