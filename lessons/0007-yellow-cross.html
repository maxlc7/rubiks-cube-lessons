Rubik's Cube · Lesson 7

The Yellow Cross

Two layers done. Now the last layer begins — and for the first time, you read the case before you act.

Where you are: white face solved, both lower layers solid, and only the yellow top remains with its four yellow edges scattered. Keep white down, yellow up and don't flip the cube again.

Why this lesson? The last layer is the heart of the mission — cube literacy. You stop following a fixed recipe and start diagnosing: you look at the top, recognize which of a few known shapes you're in, set it up, and fire the matching algorithm. This is the first true "read, then act" skill. We start with the easiest read: the yellow cross.
The Big Idea

Right now you only care about the four yellow edges — getting yellow to face up so they form a + on top. Ignore the corners and ignore the side colors. One algorithm, applied to the right shape, does it.

This is the white cross again — upside down and one rule simpler. Same shape (a +), but here you only need yellow facing up; you do not match the side colors yet (that's a later "permute" step). It's pure orientation work — the place-vs-facing split from Lesson 1, now center stage.

Read the case: Dot, L, or Line

Look straight down at the yellow top. Count only the edges (the 4 middle-of-a-side stickers, not the corners). You'll be in exactly one of these:1

Gray = not-yet-yellow. The center is always yellow, so it never counts toward the read.

The one algorithm

The same sequence handles all three cases — you just hold the cube the right way first:1

The cross-maker
F R U R' U' F'

How to hold each case, then fire

L: point the two yellow edges
to the back & left (top-left)
→
LINE: lay it flat left-to-right
(horizontal)
  • Dot (only the center): just run FRUR'U'F' once. It turns the dot into an L or a Line. Then re-read and go again.
  • L / hook: rotate the whole top (or the cube) so the two yellow edges point to the top-left (one toward the back, one toward the left), then run it once → cross.
  • Line: turn it so it lies horizontal (left-to-right), then run it once → cross.

So the worst case is just: dot → (run) → L or line → (re-hold, run) → cross. Two or three goes, max.

Why it works — and yes, the sexy move is hiding in it. Look: F ( R U R' U' ) F' — that's R U R' U' wrapped in an F … F'. The F tips the top-front edges down onto the front; the sexy move shuffles them; F' tips them back up. The round trip flips the orientation of the edges it grabbed — yellow now faces up — without dragging them out of place. Pure orientation, no permutation. And because it's a last-layer algorithm, your two solved layers below don't move.1

Hands-on: make the yellow cross ~5 min · last layer begins

  1. White down, yellow up. Look straight at the top and read your case: dot, L, or line.
  2. Set it up — L to the top-left, line horizontal. (Dot needs no setup.)
  3. Run F R U R' U' F' once.
  4. Re-read. Not a full cross yet? Identify the new case, re-hold, run again. Repeat to the cross.
  5. Yellow + on top. Don't worry that the yellow edges' side colors don't match yet — we line those up in the next stage. Right now, the goal is just the yellow plus sign.

What you just learned: the core last-layer loop — read the shape, set it up, fire the algorithm, re-read. Every remaining stage works exactly like this.

Snag — I ran it and it looks messier, not better

That's usually a setup miss, not a wrong algorithm. The L must point to the top-left and the Line must be horizontal before you start. If you fired from the wrong angle, just re-read your current case, re-hold it correctly, and run again — it always converges to the cross. The two layers underneath are safe; this algorithm puts them back every time.
