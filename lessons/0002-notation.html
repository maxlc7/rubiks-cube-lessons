Rubik's Cube · Lesson 2

The Language of Moves

Six letters and two marks. Once you can name a move, you can reason about it.

From Lesson 1: the cube is 20 movable pieces (12 edges, 8 corners) riding on 6 fixed centers, and solving means getting each piece home and facing the right way. Hold that lens — we now need a way to name the turns that move those pieces.

Why this lesson? Your mission is to understand why a sequence of moves does what it does — like "this triggers a 3-cycle of corners and touches nothing else." You can't see that pattern until you can write the moves down. Notation is the alphabet. By the end of this 6-minute lesson you'll read and perform any move sequence — and you'll discover, with your own hands, that a short sequence is a controlled, repeatable change to the cube. That discovery is the doorway to every algorithm.
The Big Idea

Each letter names one face, and turning that face a quarter-turn clockwise — clockwise as if that face were staring you in the eye.

The cube "unfolded" flat. Each letter points to the face in that position relative to you — F is whatever's facing you, U is the top, and so on.

A bare letter — R — means: grab the right layer and turn it a quarter-turn (90°) clockwise. The catch that trips everyone up: clockwise is judged from the perspective of looking straight at that face. So R sends the front-right column upward; but L — clockwise as seen from the left — sends the front-left column downward. They mirror each other.

Same face (the red Right face), three amounts of turn. The curved arrow points the direction; "clockwise" is judged as if you're staring straight at that face.

That's the entire system. Six letters × three flavours (plain, prime, 2) = 18 possible single moves, and every solve on Earth — beginner or world-record — is just a string of these.2

The prime is just "undo." R then R' leaves the cube exactly as it was — you turned one way, then back. This sounds trivial, but it's the secret engine of algorithms: solvers build sequences that mostly cancel themselves out, so only a tiny, predictable handful of pieces end up changed. You're about to feel that for yourself.

Hands-on: feel a move become a machine 4 min · the real win

Start from your solved cube. Keep it in a fixed orientation the whole time — white on top is fine; don't rotate the whole cube between moves, only turn the named face.

  1. Name one move. Do a single R. Watch which pieces left home: 4 corners and 4 edges of the right face moved; everything else — and every center — stayed. One letter, one clean quarter-turn.
  2. Undo it. Now do R'. Solved again. You just proved R and R' are exact opposites.
  3. The big one — the "sexy move." Perform this 4-move sequence, slowly, reading it left to right:
    R U R' U'
    The cube is now slightly scrambled — just a few pieces are out. Good. Don't fix it.
  4. Repeat the exact same four moves again. And again. Keep going. Count how many times total you perform R U R' U'.
  5. Watch what happens at the 6th repetition. The cube returns — perfectly solved. Six times through, and you're home.

What you just proved: a written sequence isn't random — it's a machine with a fixed period. R U R' U' cycles a small set of pieces and, repeated 6 times, brings every one of them back home.3 That is exactly what an "algorithm" is: a named string of moves with a known, controlled effect. Notation is what let you see it.

Quick check

Reason it out — instant feedback below each.

Read the language 🔤

1. What does U' tell you to do?
2. You just did F. What single move puts everything back exactly as it was?
3. Why does repeating R U R' U' eventually solve the cube again, instead of scrambling it more and more?
Where this is heading. Now that you can name moves, the next question is the heart of your mission: how does a sequence move pieces in a predictable pattern? The answer is two ideas — permutation (which pieces swap places) and cycles (pieces chasing each other around a loop, like the 6-period machine you just felt). Once you can read a sequence as "a 3-cycle of these corners, nothing else," you've crossed from doing the cube to understanding it. That's Lesson 3.
