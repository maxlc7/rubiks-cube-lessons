  1. Last layer: two corners and two edges swapped (the "out-of-order" case)

Two corners and two edges swapped

What you're seeing

The whole top is yellow and the bottom (white) is solved, plus one side face is fully done — but two or three other faces each have 1–2 pieces in the wrong spot. Look closely and it's a very specific pattern: two adjacent corners are swapped with each other, and two adjacent edges are swapped too. Everything is oriented (yellow facing up), just sitting in the wrong places.

2 corners swapped   2 edges swapped

Top is all yellow (oriented), but the red-ringed corner pair and the green-ringed edge pair are each swapped — a parity case no 3-cycle can fix.

Why the simple moves can't fix it

This is a parity thing. Your two workhorse moves — the corner positioner (U R U' L' U R' U' L) and the edge cycle — are both 3-cycles. A 3-cycle can never produce a single 2-swap. So when corners and edges are each swapped in pairs, no amount of 3-cycling fixes it. This state happens when the last-layer steps get done out of order — e.g. twisting the corners yellow-up before the pieces were fully positioned. You need one algorithm that moves a corner pair and an edge pair together.

The fix — two steps

Step 1 · Fix the corners. Hold the cube so a fully-solved side face is on your LEFT (yellow stays on top). Then run the corner-permutation algorithm once:1

Corner permutation (swaps the right-hand corner pair + an edge pair)
R U R' U' R' F R2 U' R' U' R U R' F'
chunks: (R U R′ U′) · (R′ F R2 U′) · (R′ U′) · (R U R′ F′)

All four corners should now be home — yellow up, side colors matching.

Step 2 · Fix the edges (if any are still off). Find a top edge that's already correct, turn the whole cube so that solved edge is at the BACK, then run the edge 3-cycle:1

Edge 3-cycle (slides the other three top edges)
R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2
chunks: (R U′) (R U) (R U) (R U′) · R′ U′ R2

Re-check; if a couple edges are still cycled the wrong way, run it once more (solved edge at the back each time).

You can't break it. Both algorithms only shuffle the top layer — your first two layers and any solved side face stay put no matter what. So if a setup is slightly off, you just land on a different last-layer case; re-read it and go again. Nothing below the top is ever at risk.

How to avoid it next time

Do the last layer in order: yellow cross → match the cross edges to the sides (permute edges) → position the corners → twist the corners yellow-up. Permuting the edges before the corners keeps you out of this parity trap.
