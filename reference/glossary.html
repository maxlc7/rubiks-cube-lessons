Glossary

The canonical vocabulary for this course. Terms get added as we go.

Piece (cubie) core
One of the physical little cubes that make up the puzzle. A piece carries its stickers as a fixed unit — turning a face moves whole pieces, never individual stickers. A 3×3 has 26 cubies, but only 20 move (the 6 centers are fixed).

Pieces are told apart by how many stickers they carry — 1, 2, or 3.

Center piece type
A piece with 1 sticker. There are 6, one per face, bolted to the internal core. They never change position relative to each other, so the center's color defines that face's color. Anchor of the whole cube.
center 1 sticker · fixed

The green-ringed sticker is the whole piece — it never leaves its face.

Edge piece type
A piece with 2 stickers, sitting between two centers. There are 12. Has a home slot determined by its two colors, and a correct orientation.
edge 2 stickers

Two colors meet on one piece — white + red here.

Corner piece type
A piece with 3 stickers, at the cube's 8 corners. There are 8. The trickiest type because a corner can be in its home slot yet twisted the wrong way.
corner 3 stickers

Three colors on one piece — white + red + green here.

Permutation (placement) core
Where a piece is — which slot it occupies. A piece is "permuted correctly" when it's in its home slot, regardless of which way it faces.
right place in its home slot

Permutation is only which slot a piece sits in — see Orientation for the other half.

Orientation core
Which way a piece is turned within its slot. A piece is fully solved only when it is both permuted (right place) and oriented (right way around). This place-vs-facing distinction is the backbone of last-layer solving.
solved right place + right way
vs
twisted right place, wrong way

Same home slot both times. The red-ringed sticker points the wrong way — placed but not oriented.

Two ways to name a face core
By position (U/D/F/B/R/L) = where a face points relative to you — these change when you rotate the whole cube. By color = the center's color, which is bolted to the core and never changes — the "white face" is always the white-center face. Position names move; color names are permanent identity.
Notation (U D F B R L) core
The move language. Each letter = a face relative to you: Up, Down, Front, Back, Right, Left. A bare letter means turn that face a quarter-turn (90°) clockwise — as seen looking straight at that face. (J Perm, Move Notation)

Color scheme & opposite pairs reference
Standard (Western) cube. The 6 centers sit in fixed opposite pairs that can never separate: White ↔ Yellow, Blue ↔ Green, Red ↔ Orange. So with white up, yellow is always down; with blue in front, green is always at the back. Massimo's cube, working setup: White=Up, Yellow=Down, Orange=Front, Red=Back, Green=Right, Blue=Left. (Position labels move if you turn the whole cube; the opposite pairs never do.)

Prime ( ' ) and double ( 2 ) notation
R' ("R prime") = quarter-turn counter-clockwise — the exact undo of R. R2 = half-turn (180°), direction irrelevant. Six faces × three flavours = the 18 basic moves; every solve is a string of these.
Matched (true) cross core
The real goal of Stage 1 — not just four white stickers forming a + on the white face, but each white edge's second color also matching the center beside it. A "white cross" with mismatched side colors is a fake cross: it looks done but is built crooked, and every later stage inherits the error. A piece is only home when ALL its colors are right.

✗

Each column = an edge's side color (top) over its center (bottom). True = the side color matches its centre (left, ✓); fake = mismatch (right, ✗).

Daisy technique
The foolproof way to build a matched cross. Hold yellow on top / white on bottom and gather the four white edges as "petals" around the yellow center (white facing up), ignoring side colors. Then, one at a time: turn the top until a petal's side color sits above its matching center, and turn that face 180° to drop the edge straight down — matched before the drop, so matched after. Each edge uses a different face, so placed edges never collide. (J Perm, the cross)

Top-down view — four white "petals" around the yellow centre (gray = corners, ignored).

Algorithm core
A named, written string of moves with a known, controlled effect on the pieces — not a random scramble. Most algorithms mostly cancel themselves out so only a small, predictable set of pieces changes. Example: the "sexy move" R U R' U' has order 6 — repeat it six times and a solved cube returns to solved.
God's Number theory
20 — the proven maximum number of face turns needed to solve any of the cube's ~43 quintillion positions (half-turn metric). The theoretical "quickest worst case." Humans don't solve optimally; we use memorable methods instead. (cube20.org)
